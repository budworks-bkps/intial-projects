Sailing through
bearish markets

The ongoing US–Iran conflict has kept markets on edge. Over the past few days, NIFTY has slipped significantly. It closed at 24,865.70 on March 2 and closed at 23,002.15 on March 19, that means a 7.5% decrease in 21 days.

During past geopolitical conflicts, markets have typically seen short-term corrections but strong recoveries over the following year. 

Markets after wars & global conflicts

After World War II, US markets went on to see one of their strongest multi-year bull runs.

Following the Gulf War in 1991, markets bounced back sharply within months.

When the Iraq War began in 2003, equities recovered and moved into a strong rally.

Even during more recent geopolitical tensions, markets have usually stabilised and moved higher once uncertainty started easing.

Why does this happen?

Markets are forward-looking.

They react fast to fear, but also quickly factor in recovery, rebuilding, and a return to normal.

Conflicts can drive short-term volatility, but long-term growth has usually stayed on track.

A note from history, to long-
term investors:

Volatility is temporary.

Markets have continued to grow over decades despite wars, crises, and recessions.

 

Compounding needs time.

Wealth builds over years, not weeks or months.

 

Staying invested helps.

Timing market tops and bottoms is extremely difficult, even for experts.

So if you’re wondering whether to hold or sell, choose what gives you the most peace of mind. But try not to make decisions purely out of panic.

History shows that markets have gone through many uncertain times before and recovered.
